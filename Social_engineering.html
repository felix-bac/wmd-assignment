Hackers Increasingly Leverage Social Engineering to Breach Systems

By Cybersecurity Correspondent

April 30, 2025

As cyber defenses become increasingly sophisticated, hackers are increasingly relying on an old trick with frightening success: social engineering. This human psychology-based method of attack, rather than one based on technical weaknesses, has taken off, allowing cybercrooks to outsmart even the best security measures. Phishing emails and sophisticated pretexting attacks are just the beginning, as social engineering assaults become the hallmark of contemporary hacking attacks, aimed at individuals, corporations, and governments all over the globe.

The Rise of Social Engineering

Social engineering is utilized to manipulate individuals into divulging confidential information or performing actions that would compromise security, such as clicking on spam links or sending login credentials. In a 2024 Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report, 68% of data breaches involved a non-malicious human element, where social engineering was a key contributor. The tactic's success in carrying out attacks stems from its ability to harness trust, fear, or curiosity, making it a low-cost, high-yield attack vector for adversaries.

"Social engineering is the path of least resistance," says Dr. Lydia Kostopoulos, who specializes in human factors and cyber security. "Why spend weeks exploiting a zero-day vulnerability when you can cause an employee to surrender access within minutes?"

Phishing remains the favorite social engineering attack method, with attackers using misleading emails or text messages that pretend to be from reputable institutions such as banks, IT personnel, or colleagues. More advanced techniques are becoming increasingly common, though. Pretexting, in which hackers make up stories to pilfer information, and vishing (voice phishing) scams using AI-generated voices are on the upswing. In 2024, Google saw a 35% rise in spear-phishing attacks targeting high-priority targets like executives and IT administrators.

High-Profile Cases Emphasize the Danger

Current instances point to greater reliance on social engineering. At the beginning of 2025, a major U.S. health care system was hit by a breach when an employee was phished into an e-mail purporting to be an update on payroll, exposing the personal data of 1.2 million patients. A European bank also lost €50 million after a finance officer was phished into a vishing call that impersonated the CEO of the company, complete with a deepfake voice.

Hackers are also using today's events to reinforce their tactics. In the 2024 U.S. presidential election, cybercriminals used phishing emails as fake campaign donation requests to voters with malware-infested links. The COVID-19 pandemic reported an increase in impersonation scams by health organizations, a pattern that is still ongoing with promises of fake vaccine certificates.

Why Social Engineering Works

Social engineering is successful due to the exploitation of human vulnerabilities. Attackers capitalize on feelings like urgency, fear, or greed and set up situations that lead to immediate, reflexive responses. One example is a widely used phishing attack where users are threatened that their account will be deactivated if they do not verify their credentials in a matter of seconds. AI tools have escalated this threat because hackers can use them to generate highly targeted messages by scraping social media profiles or public data breaches.

"AI made social engineering so much more dangerous," Secureworks threat intelligence analyst Mark Thompson told Threatpost. "Attackers can now automatically carry out attacks at scale by leveraging data in LinkedIn or X to craft messages that look uncomfortably personalized."

Also, the shift to remote work has created new vectors of attack. Remote employees tend to be less careful regarding security protocols, and the lack of in-person verification gives hackers more opportunities to impersonate co-workers or IT staff. A report by IBM Security 2024 found that 43% of companies had been compromised through remote work setups, and social engineering was a top vector.

Evolving Tactics and Tools

Hackers are adopting increasingly sophisticated methods to enhance their social engineering campaigns:

Deepfake Technology: AI-generated voices and videos are used in vishing and video conferencing scams to impersonate trusted figures.

Business Email Compromise (BEC): Attackers infiltrate corporate email systems to send fraudulent payment requests, costing businesses $2.7 billion globally in 2024, per the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.

QR Code Attacks: Public-place or email-based malicious QR codes trick victims into visiting fake login pages, a tactic that jumped 50% in 2024, according to Proofpoint.

Multi-Channel Attacks: Hackers using email, SMS, and calls to create urgency and make the victim act swiftly increase the success rate.

Ransomware groups, such as LockBit and Conti, have also added social engineering to their playbooks. Before deploying malware, these groups will often contact employees, posing as recruiters or vendors, to gather intelligence or push malicious payloads.

Fighting the Human Factor

Organizations are struggling to counter this growing threat. Traditional cybersecurity defenses, including firewalls and antivirus software, are ineffective against social engineering, which targets people, not systems. Experts suggest a multi-layered approach:

Employee Training: Interactive, ongoing training in recognizing phishing, vishing, and pretexting scams is necessary. Simulations, like mock phishing emails, can raise awareness.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): MFA may prevent unauthorized access even when credentials are stolen, although attackers now also aim for MFA fatigue by bombarding users with authentication requests.

Zero Trust Architecture: Verifying every user and device, wherever they might be, reduces the likelihood of compromised accounts.

Incident Reporting: Encouraging employees to report suspicious behavior without fear of reprisal can catch attacks early.

Governments are stepping up efforts as well. In 2024, the United States Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) launched a public campaign to educate the population on phishing and QR code fraud. The European Union's Cybersecurity Agency (ENISA) has proposed stricter regulation on AI-created content to block deepfake-enabled attacks.

The Road Ahead

As hackers become more skilled at social engineering, the cybersecurity community is up against a difficult fight. The weakest link in any security chain is the human factor, and no amount of technology can remove the threat of manipulation entirely. Organizations need to create a skeptical culture where employees are wary of unsolicited requests and authenticate identities via secure avenues.

"Social engineering thrives on trust," Kostopoulos said. "The best defense is to educate people to trust their gut and check before they click."

As the threat landscape continues to evolve, staying one step ahead of social engineering will require a combination of technology, education, and awareness. Otherwise, hackers will continue to exploit the one vulnerability that can't be patched: human nature.
